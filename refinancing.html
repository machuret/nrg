In plain English

What refinancing actually means.

Refinancing just means swapping your current home loan for a better one — sometimes with your existing lender, more often with a different one. Same house, same mortgage, just better terms.

People do it for a sharper rate, to unlock equity for a reno or a deposit, to roll other debts in, or to switch features like an offset account. No jargon, no catch.

The trick isn’t chasing the lowest number on a comparison site — it’s working out whether the saving is real once you account for fees, your remaining term, and what you actually need the loan to do. That’s the bit we do for you.

The questions we get asked

Good questions. Straight answers.

Is it worth refinancing right now?

It depends on your rate, your balance and how long you have left. If you haven’t reviewed your loan in a few years, there’s a good chance it’s worth a look — and we’ll tell you straight whether the saving actually beats the switching costs. No saving, no switch. Simple.

What does it actually cost to switch loans?

Usually a discharge fee from your current lender and a small settlement and registration cost with the new one — often just a few hundred dollars all up. Plenty of lenders offer cashback or waive fees to win your business. We weigh the real cost against the saving before you commit to anything.

Will refinancing hurt my credit score?

An application leaves a credit enquiry, which can nudge your score short-term, but it recovers. We only lodge with a lender once we’re confident it’s the right fit — so you’re not firing off applications all over the place chasing a number.

How long does it take?

Most refinances settle within two to four weeks once your paperwork is in. We handle the lender chase, the forms and the settlement, and keep you posted at every step so you’re never left wondering.
